THE MONTY HALL PROBLEM

The Monty Hall problem, named after the host of the American television game show "Let's Make a Deal," has captivated mathematicians, statisticians, and enthusiasts since it emerged. Despite its apparent simplicity, this probability puzzle defies intuition and challenges our understanding of chances.

The Game

Here's how the gameshow went down:

There are 3 doors, two of them with a goat behind it, one with a car.

1. The contestant picks whatever door they think might have the car.

2. The host opens a door with a goat, thus eliminating one losing door.

3. The contestant is given the option to switch to the other unopened door or to stay with the chosen door



At this point, most of us would probably just go with out gut feeling or even be inclined to stay with our initial choice. That's not a good strategy, however, if you want the odds to be in your favour.



A Question from a Reader and a controversial answer

In 1990, a reader submitted the following question to the magazine "Parade" asking their inhouse genius, Marilyn Von Savant:

So, in short, Mr. Whitaker wanted to know- does it make a difference if you switch doors on "Let's Make a Deal"?

Marilyn Von Savant had a somewhat surprising answer to this question:

Her answer is that you double your winning chances if you switch to the other unopened door when the hosts makes the offer.

If you chose a door out of a million where 999.999 had goats and only one had a car, your odds would obviously not be good. However, if the host opened all the doors, but one, and gave you a choice between that and your chosen one, you'd probably switch. It was one out 999.999, after all.

Although it is an explaination, it still doesn't quite make sense to most of use.

With 1 car and 1 goat and 2 doors, it seems to be a 50/50 case, and the fact that the host eliminated a goat door shouldn't really affect the odds.

The Final Solution

As it turned out, the response to the readers question in Parade was completely correct- you should definitely switch doors if given the option.

This bit of statistics is a counter intuitive as they get. The fact remains, however, that the initially chosen door has a 1/3 chance of winning, the second one has a 2/3 chance, although most of us would say it was the same odds for both doors. Another very odd thing about this problem is that if the host chose at random and happened to chose a goat door, it would indeed be 50/50. Very strange.

At least the solution can be calculated using something called "Bayes' theorem".

Bayes' Theorem allows us to calculate the probability of an event based on prior knowledge of conditions related to the event. The formula is:


\( P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)} \)

Where:

  • P(A|B) is the probability of event A occurring given that event B has occurred.
  • P(B|A) is the probability of event B occurring given that event A has occurred.
  • P(A) is the prior probability of event A.
  • P(B) is the prior probability of event B.
