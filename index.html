THE MONTY HALL PROBLEM

The Monty Hall problem, named after the host of the American television game show "Let's Make a Deal," has captivated mathematicians, statisticians, and enthusiasts since it emerged. Despite its apparent simplicity, this probability puzzle defies intuition and challenges our understanding of chances.

A Question from a Reader and a controversial answer

In 1990, a reader submitted the following question to the magazine "Parade" asking their inhouse genius, Marilyn Von Savant:

So, in short, does it make a difference if you switch doors on "Let's Make a Deal"?

Marilyn Von Savant had a somewhat surprising answer to this question:

So switch and double your chances? With 1 car and 1 goat left surely it doesn't matter if you switch doors? Logic tells us that each door had a 1/3 chance of holding the car when the game started. After the host eliminated a goat door, the odds must be 1/2 for each remaining door. Either your chosen door has the car or the other door has it, a 50/50 case. Right?

The Final Solution

As it turned out, the response to the readers question in Parade was completely correct- you should definitely switch doors if given the option.

This bit of statistics is a counter intuitive as they get. The fact remains, however, that the chosen door intially had a 1/3 chance of having a car behind it and those odds stay the same throughout the game. The solution can even be calculated using something called "Bayes' theorem".

Bayes' Theorem allows us to calculate the probability of an event based on prior knowledge of conditions related to the event. The formula is:

\( P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)} \)

Where:

  • P(A|B) is the probability of event A occurring given that event B has occurred.
  • P(B|A) is the probability of event B occurring given that event A has occurred.
  • P(A) is the prior probability of event A.
  • P(B) is the prior probability of event B.
