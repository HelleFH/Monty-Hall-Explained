THE MONTY HALL PROBLEM

The Monty Hall problem, named after the host of the American television game show "Let's Make a Deal," has captivated mathematicians, statisticians, and enthusiasts since it emerged. Despite its apparent simplicity, this probability puzzle defies intuition and challenges our understanding of chances.

Here's how the gameshow went down:

There are 3 doors, two of them with a goat behind it, one with a car.

Step 1

The contestant picks whatever door they think might have the car.

Step 2

The host opens a door with a goat, thus eliminating one losing door.

Step 3

The contestant is given the option to switch to the other unopened door.

At this point, most of us would probably just go with our gut feeling or even be inclined to stay with our initial choice. That's not a good strategy, however, if you want the odds to be in your favor.

In 1990, a reader submitted the following question to the magazine "Parade" asking their in-house genius, Marilyn Von Savant:

So, in short, Mr. Whitaker wanted to know: does it make a difference if you switch doors on "Let's Make a Deal"?

Marilyn Von Savant had a somewhat surprising answer to this question:

Her answer is that you double your winning chances if you switch to the other unopened door when the host makes the offer.

Although it is an explanation, it still doesn't quite make sense to most of us.

With 1 car and 1 goat and 2 doors, it seems to be a 50/50 case, and the fact that the host eliminated a goat door shouldn't really affect the odds.

This was the general consensus in the world of maths and statistics, and Marilyn’s answer generated a lot of heat.

Marilyn persisted, however. And, as the months went on, simulations were run and numbers double-checked, and some very different responses began to emerge.

As it turned out, the response to the reader's question in Parade was completely correct—you should definitely switch doors if given the option.

This bit of statistics is as counterintuitive as they get. The fact remains, however, that the initially chosen door has a 1/3 chance of winning, while the second one has a 2/3 chance, although most of us would say it was the same odds for both doors.

Another very odd thing about this problem is that if the host chose at random and happened to choose a goat door, it would indeed be 50/50. Very strange.

At least the solution can be calculated using something called "Bayes' theorem".


\( P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)} \)

Where:

  • P(A|B) is the probability of event A occurring given that event B has occurred.
  • P(B|A) is the probability of event B occurring given that event A has occurred.
  • P(A) is the probability of event A occurring.
  • P(B) is the probability of event B occurring.
